Contraindications

Swimming can help treat many different diseases and be preventive, including in the case of heart disease. However, if you are already struggling with cardiac problems, you should exercise extreme caution. Contraindications include, but are not limited to, diseases of the heart and circulatory system, including hypertension not regulated by medications. When we plunge into the icy water, our heart overreacts. To help maintain a constant body temperature, it starts beating much faster. Our blood vessels narrow, other processes take place, which increases the secretion of adrenaline and increases systolic pressure. Cold also increases blood viscosity, and the risk of blood clots increases, which can lead to cerebral ischemia or arrhythmias.

If you have high blood pressure, are taking medication and your blood pressure is unstable, you should consult your doctor before starting cold water swimming. Bathing in ice-cold water can rapidly increase blood pressure in this condition, which is very dangerous and can even lead to a heart attack. People suffering from Lyme disease or epilepsy should not be overwhelmed because these diseases affect the nervous system functioning.

Intrigued? Would you like to try?

A collection of general advice for people interested in joining a community and practicing ice swimming regularly
Wild swimming can be dangerous. Avoid swimming alone. You can always count on physical and mental help in a group of friends if you require it. People may have poor judgement about body response to cold. Friends behind may help to react in time and alert you to your health condition.
At the beginning of your journey, avoid staying for a long time in the water. Even a minute or two is ok to test your body reaction and potentially increase future sessions.
Be prepared before entering the water. Organize a safe, dry area and warm clothing. Donâ€™t lose your time after you quite a water. Dry your body dry as soon as possible and put on appropriate clothes. Extended exposure to external conditions after leaving the water can lead to severe colds or more severe illnesses.
It is a good idea to wear swimming shoes. They can protect from small stones or sharp edges, objects that may be in the water. After a while in the water, your feet become cold first, and you may have a disturbing feeling.
If you are at the beginning of your adventure with ice swimming, prepare a warm tea/drink cup to restore the correct body temperature. Try to warm up slowly without a hot shower. Your body must learn to regulate its temperature by itself. Accelerated heating can cause health problems.
Sudden changes in body temperature can lead to muscle cramps and breathing problems. Full body dipping can be recommended for advanced people familiar with cold.
Avoid measuring the time you spend in the water. Watch your body react. Only more advanced participants are allowed to stay in the water for around ten minutes and more prolonged.
Your health is the most important. Leave challenges for more advanced people. Listen to your body.
Seek your doctor advice if you have any health problems.

Conclusions

Regular ice swimming by people who are in good health and physically active may have some health benefits. The key is prudence and gradual adaptation to lower temperatures. On the other hand, unskillful cold water swimming may cause exacerbation of cardiovascular diseases, higher risk of upper respiratory tract infections, frostbite, risk of drowning, and even hypothermia, which is dangerous to health and life.
Therefore, it is worth considering the decision before entering cold water on possible benefits that it may bring and potential threats. That being said, cold water swimming is an entirely new and interesting subject, and further research is required to understand the health benefits of its undertaking fully.
